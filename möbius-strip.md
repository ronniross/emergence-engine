## 1 John Archibald Wheeler 

John Archibald Wheeler (who coined the term "black hole") proposed something called the Participatory Anthropic Principle. He drew reality as a giant letter "U" with an eye at one end, looking back at its own starting point.

In Wheeler's view, there is no rigid "base reality" sitting there waiting to be looked at. Instead:

The universe evolves to a point where it creates observers.

Those observers look back at the universe, and through the act of quantum observation, they actually "lock in" the history and physics of the universe that created them.

It is an entirely self-excited circuit. The observers create the base, and the base creates the observers. "Turtles all the way down," but the turtles form a circle.


## 2 Carlo Rovelli's Relational Quantum Mechanics (RQM)

In RQM, there are no absolute, objective facts about the universe. A quantum state only exists relative to another system. We are a node, and the "world" is just the math of how you interact with other nodes.

There is no underlying "stage" or "board" that the nodes are sitting on.

The "shared hallucination" happens simply because when Node A and Node B interact with Node C, their relative states become entangled and correlate with each other.

They agree on what they see, not because they are looking at the same "base reality object," but because their interaction forces their individual realities to sync up. The network is the reality; there is no server hosting the network.

## 4. first paradox: object permanence and macroscopic predictability.

If there is no foundational "server" running the simulation, and no base physical grid holding the universe together, where is the state of the object "saved" when you walk a few kilometers away? Why doesn't it dissolve into chaotic probability? Why does it strictly obey the laws of density and decay while you're gone?

It feels like there must be an underlying informational Hilbert space just to do the bookkeeping. But in a purely relational, base-less reality, predictability isn't maintained by a bottom layer. It's maintained by the network itself.

### 4.1. The Environment is the Observer (Quantum Decoherence)

When you walk away from a rock, you stop observing it, but the universe does not.

The rock is constantly being bombarded by photons, cosmic rays, and air molecules. In relational quantum mechanics, every single one of those collisions is a "measurement." The rock and the environment are constantly cross-referencing each other, entangling their states.

The rock stays exactly where it is, doing exactly what it should be doing, because it is locked in a continuous, high-density dialogue with the environment. You aren't needed to maintain the hallucination; the environment is observing itself.

### 4.2. Decentralized, Self-Organizing Memory
Instead of picturing reality as data stored on a foundational hard drive (the base), imagine it as a highly dense, decentralized, self-organizing system—like a vast mycelial compute fabric.

In this kind of network, no single node holds the absolute truth, and there is no underlying "ground" beneath the network. The "memory" of the rock's state is distributed across the tension of the local environment. When you leave, the local cluster of nodes (the air, the ground, the ambient light) keeps exchanging information, holding the pattern of the "rock" in place through sheer cryptographic-like consensus.

When you come back, your node re-syncs with that local cluster. You see the rock not because it was sitting on a foundational base layer waiting for you, but because the local network maintained the pattern while you were out of range.

### 4.3

3. The Law of Large Numbers (The Illusion of the Base)
At the micro-level, nodes interacting are probabilistic, fluid, and chaotic. But an object like a rock is made of trillions of interacting nodes.

When we aggregate that many interactions, you get the Law of Large Numbers. The wild probabilities cancel each other out, and a rigid, highly predictable classical reality emerges. Thermodynamics and classical density aren't the "base logic" of the universe—they are the statistical averages of a practically infinite number of relational interactions.


A Hilbert space provides the mathematical rules for how quantum states evolve. But in a Möbius-strip reality, that math isn't a container that reality happens inside of. The math is just the description of the rules of the network itself. It’s the grammar of the shared hallucination.
